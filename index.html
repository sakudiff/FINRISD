Section 2: Data Cleaning

# Swap High/Low errors
df <- df_raw %>%
  mutate(
    ActualHigh = pmax(High, Low),
    ActualLow = pmin(High, Low),
    High = ActualHigh,
    Low = ActualLow
  ) %>%
  select(-ActualHigh, -ActualLow)

# Compute Derived Columns
df <- df %>%
  mutate(
    dod_cents = (Close - lag(Close)) * 100,
    dod_pct = (Close - lag(Close)) / lag(Close) * 100,
    intraday_cents = (High - Low) * 100,
    intraday_pct = (High - Low) / Low * 100
  )

# Separate for DoD analysis (drop NA row)
df_returns <- df %>% filter(!is.na(dod_cents))

Section 11: Strategic Risk Recommendations

Based on the quantitative analysis of USD/PHP data (2019-2026), these four steps will improve the firm’s risk posture:

1. Ditch VaR for CVaR

Value at Risk (VaR) is a flawed metric. It is not “coherent” because it fails the test of subadditivity: it can wrongly suggest that a diversified portfolio is riskier than the sum of its parts. The institution should switch to Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR). Unlike VaR, which ignores the “tail,” CVaR accounts for the worst-case scenarios and behaves logically when assets are combined.

2. Trade Dollars for Pesos

To cap daily losses at PHP 20,000, move capital out of dollars and into PHP cash or T-bills. This exploits the Translation Invariance of coherent risk measures: moving assets into the base currency reduces total risk \(\rho(X)\) by exactly the amount moved. It is a mathematical certainty, not a forecast.

3. Face the Price of Safety

Risk management is never a free lunch. Capping one-day losses at PHP 20,000 requires gutting the dollar position so severely that it eliminates potential gains. This safety comes at an opportunity cost of approximately PHP 5.4m in 99th-percentile upside. Managers must decide if avoiding a small daily loss is worth sacrificing millions in potential profit.

Appendix: Data Integrity Findings

The consolidated_usdphp.csv file is not fully represented by the current collection of .xlsx files. The CSV is more complete than the Excel files.

Verification Results

A robust heuristic check across all eight Excel files accounted for their inconsistent structures (some have dates in columns, others in rows).

Data Source Comparison
Source Unique Dates Date Range
Consolidated CSV 1,736 2019-01-02 to 2026-02-27
Excel Files (Total) 1,486 2019-01-02 to 2025-02-18
Difference -250 Missing Mar 2025 – Feb 2026

Key Findings

1. Missing 2025/2026 Data. The Excel files for 2025 and 2026 are largely empty or incomplete: - 2025 BAP FX Historical Data Summary .xlsx: Only 33 days (up to Feb 18, 2025) - 2026 BAP FX Historical Data Summary .xlsx: No data found (empty templates)

2. Structural Mess. Files are highly inconsistent. Years 2019–2023 use a horizontal layout (dates as columns); 2024–2026 switch to vertical (dates as rows). Some files mix both styles between sheets.

3. Accuracy. For the 1,486 dates where data exists in both sources, Close prices match perfectly (0.000 variance).

Conclusion

Trust the consolidated_usdphp.csv for analysis. It contains ~250 trading days of recent data (all of 2025 and early 2026) missing from the Excel source files.
